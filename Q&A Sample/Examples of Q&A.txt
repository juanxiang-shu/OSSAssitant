This can occur through different mechanisms depending on the nature of the system and the available energy sources. Thermal activation involves heating the sample to increase the average translational or vibrational energy of the reactants, thereby increasing their collision frequency and overcoming the barrier through thermal fluctuations. Photoexcitation uses light to promote electrons from occupied states to unoccupied ones, creating hot carriers that can directly inject into adsorbate orbitals or indirectly populate excited states via electron-electron or electron-phonon coupling. Tunneling electron excitation involves injecting electrons from an external source (e.g., STM tip) into the molecule or substrate, which can either transiently populate antibonding orbitals or trigger nonadiabatic transitions if the energy exceeds the threshold. Each method has distinct advantages: thermal activation is gentle and widely applicable but slow; photoexcitation offers ultrafast control and high selectivity but requires specific photon energies; tunneling excitation provides atomic-scale spatial resolution but is limited by tip-molecule coupling efficiency. Activation is often followed by relaxation processes such as electron-hole pair formation or phonon emission, which can lead to dissociation or desorption. The choice of activation method thus determines not only the success rate but also the final products and dynamics of the reaction. 
Answer: Activation in surface reactions refers to the process of overcoming the kinetic barrier that prevents spontaneous reaction between adsorbed species by elevating the system's energy above the activation threshold. The selection of activation method therefore represents a trade-off between controllability, efficiency, and applicability, with each finding use in specific experimental contexts. 1. Energy Perspective: Surface vs. Bulk
* Broken Symmetry and Surface Free Energy: In the bulk, every atom is fully coordinated, experiencing a symmetric environment. At a surface, this symmetry is broken〞atoms have fewer neighbors, resulting in unsaturated bonds and higher energy states. This is quantified as the surface free energy, a thermodynamic driving force for surface relaxation, reconstruction, and sometimes even spontaneous chemical reactions not seen in the bulk.
* Activation Barriers and Reaction Pathways: The energetic landscape at a surface is fundamentally different. Adsorbed species may face lower (or sometimes higher) activation energies for reaction, due to the influence of the substrate＊s electronic structure and the possibility of surface-catalyzed bond rearrangement. In contrast, in solution or bulk, the three-dimensional environment and solvation effects dominate the energy landscape.
* Thermodynamic vs. Kinetic Control: Surfaces can stabilize metastable or transition states, shifting the product distribution compared to bulk or solution-phase reactions〞sometimes making possible reactions that are unfavorable or forbidden in the bulk.
2. Structure Perspective:
* Atomic Arrangement and Reconstruction: The atomic positions at surfaces are not simple truncations of the bulk lattice. Surfaces often undergo relaxation (small shifts in atomic positions) and reconstruction (formation of new periodicities or bonding patterns), fundamentally altering local geometry and available reaction sites. These can be directly observed using LEED/RHEED (long-range order), and STM/AFM (atomic-scale structure).
* Adsorption Sites and Geometry: Surfaces present a variety of geometrically and electronically distinct adsorption sites (bridge, top, hollow, step, kink, vacancy, etc.), each with unique reactivity. This site specificity is absent in the bulk, where local environments are largely uniform.
* Confinement and Dimensionality: On-surface reactions are confined to (quasi-) two dimensions. This surface confinement restricts reactant orientation and diffusion, often leading to unique products and selectivities compared to three-dimensional bulk or solution-phase systems.
3. Application Perspective:
* Catalysis and Selectivity: Surface properties directly control the performance of catalysts, sensors, and electronic devices. Surface structure, electronic states, and even defects dictate activity, selectivity, and stability〞properties that cannot be predicted from the bulk alone.
* Nanostructure Fabrication and Function: As highlighted in your context, on-surface synthesis enables the formation of ordered nanostructures and polymers unachievable in solution due to solubility, aggregation, or lack of templating. Surface templating and dynamic covalent chemistry facilitate error correction and ordering.
* Electronic Devices and Interfaces: In many advanced applications (molecular electronics, 2D materials, heterostructures), the interface〞not the bulk〞determines key properties such as charge transport, band alignment, and device stability.
Why Can＊t Bulk Properties Suffice?
* Surface-Specific States: Electronic states at the surface (e.g., surface bands, dangling bonds, image states) can dominate interactions with adsorbates, affecting reactivity and device properties. These states are absent in the bulk.
* Chemical Environment: Surface atoms may have different oxidation states, coordination, and even chemical identity (e.g., surface segregation or contamination) compared to the bulk.
* Experimental Evidence: Techniques such as XPS, STM, and TPD repeatedly show that surface chemical shifts, adsorption energies, and reaction mechanisms cannot be extrapolated from bulk data. As your context notes, even DFT modeling must be corrected to account for differences in surface vs. bulk electronic structure.
